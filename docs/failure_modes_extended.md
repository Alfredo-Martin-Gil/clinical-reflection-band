# Failure Modes in Reflective Cognitive Artifacts

## Purpose

The Reflection Band is designed as a reflective artifact rather than a decision system.

However, when reflective systems interact with real clinical environments, a number of failure modes may emerge.

These failures are not primarily technical.  
They arise from the interaction between human cognition, organizational context, and system behavior.

Understanding these risks is essential to preserve the intended boundary of the artifact.

---

## 1. Alert Fatigue Drift

If reflective signals appear too frequently, clinicians may begin to ignore them.

Even signals that are conceptually correct lose value if they compete with attention in high-load environments.

Silence discipline is therefore essential to prevent the artifact from becoming another background alert.

---

## 2. Epistemic Overconfidence

A reflective signal that appears calm and structured may create the impression that the system has a deeper understanding than it actually does.

This is particularly dangerous when signals are generated from incomplete or low-integrity state capture.

The artifact must avoid converting uncertain input into artificially coherent reflection.

---

## 3. Silent Dependency

Over time, clinicians may become accustomed to the artifact's presence.

Even if the system does not give directions, users may subconsciously rely on its silence as reassurance.

If the system fails or is unavailable, the absence of that familiar reflective layer may alter cognitive behavior.

This phenomenon can occur even when the artifact never provided guidance.

---

## 4. Authority Inversion

If reflective signals are interpreted as protective safeguards rather than cognitive mirrors, responsibility may begin to shift implicitly from the clinician to the system.

This risk emerges when signals are framed as "error detection" rather than "tension surfacing".

Maintaining the descriptive nature of signals is critical to prevent this inversion.

---

## 5. Coherence Overclaim

A reflective signal may appear to organize the situation into a meaningful structure even when the underlying data is insufficient.

This can create a false sense of frame stability.

To prevent this, the artifact must respect the state integrity constraint and remain silent when the observed state cannot support responsible reflection.

---

## 6. Context Drift

Signals designed for one clinical environment may be interpreted differently in another.

For example, a tension that is meaningful in emergency care may be perceived as unnecessary noise in a low-pressure setting.

Reflective artifacts must therefore remain sensitive to context and cognitive load.

---

## Conceptual role of failure analysis

Documenting failure modes does not weaken the artifact.

On the contrary, it clarifies the conditions under which the system can remain conceptually disciplined and operationally useful.

Reflective systems must be designed with awareness of how easily human interpretation can drift beyond the original intent.

---

## Status

Conceptual analysis only.

No implementation or deployment assumptions are implied.
