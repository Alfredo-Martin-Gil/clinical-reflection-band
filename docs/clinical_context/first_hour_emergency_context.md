# First-Hour Emergency Context

## Why this scenario matters

The first hour of a severe medical emergency represents one of the highest cognitive-load environments in clinical medicine.

During this period:

- patient trajectory may evolve rapidly
- available data is incomplete
- interventions may have irreversible consequences
- attention is divided across multiple competing demands

In these conditions, the main limitation is often not medical knowledge but cognitive stability under pressure.

---

## Typical characteristics of the first hour

The early phase of life-threatening emergencies often combines:

- partial information
- evolving physiology
- environmental noise
- social pressure from bystanders or relatives
- limited diagnostic resources
- rapid intervention cycles

These factors produce a high-entropy decision environment.

---

## The cognitive problem

In high-entropy situations, clinicians frequently experience:

- internal re-check loops
- uncertainty about whether the current frame is coherent
- tension between available data and emerging intuition
- pressure to justify decisions in real time

The Reflection Band is designed to interact with this cognitive layer rather than the clinical decision itself.

---

## What the artifact does not do

Within the first-hour context, the artifact does not:

- generate diagnoses
- recommend treatments
- optimize clinical decisions
- replace clinical reasoning

Authority remains entirely with the clinician.

---

## What the artifact attempts to stabilize

The artifact operates only at the level of epistemic state.

It may:

- name structural tensions in the current frame
- surface coherence gaps
- highlight trajectory instability
- clarify when available data does not stabilize into a coherent interpretation

This reflection is intended to stabilize attention rather than influence direction.

---

## Example reflective signals

Possible signals within this context may look like:

Stable surface. Coherence gap present.

Stable frame. Trajectory instability detected.

Presentation exceeds current explanatory frame.

These signals describe the state of the frame without suggesting any course of action.

---

## Why silence remains dominant

Under emergency conditions, attention is a scarce resource.

For this reason the Reflection Band follows a strict rule:

If the reflective signal cannot reduce cognitive noise, it should not appear.

Silence indicates that:

- the current frame remains coherent
- no meaningful tension is detected
- or the available state does not justify reflection

---

## Conceptual interpretation

In the first hour of a critical emergency, the artifact acts as a minimal epistemic stabilizer.

It does not attempt to solve the clinical problem.

It simply helps clarify the cognitive ground from which the clinician's decision emerges.

This preserves authority while reducing perceptual ambiguity under pressure.
